Demographic analysis

2016 edition of Lausanne Marathon had a significantly higher percentage of male runners than female ones (6801 men compared to 4447 men). This gap between male and female participants is way bigger than the one in population of Canton of Vaud (52% women, 48% men) according to official study from the Swiss government. It would be interesting to determine why races of Lausanne Marathon had so many male runners compared to the average. Some information and explanations of such distinction is given by the new direction of the marathon here (RTSSport) . Among other things:

"Women don't even dare to start the race unless they are totally sure to master the distance unlike men, who sometimes finish completely exhausted."

As we can notice from the graph below, there are huge differences about the repartition of men and women following the running. Indeed, 10-km running is most approchable race for beginners and distribution between genders seems well balanced, while the gap tends to increase for semi-marathon and marathon. Notice that marathon attracts less people, as it is seen like a race for 'specialists'.

Note: The following distribution seems to be a direct consequence of the new organisation of Lausanne Marathon decided in 2009 and which limits, among other things, the budget accorded to professional runners (i.e. runners of the marathon).

Ages of runners seem to be quite normally distributed with a mean age of 38.98 years (41.65 years for male runners and 36,87 years for female runners). However, if we look closer, we notice that participants of marathon are older (with a mean of 42,47 years) while the ones of 10-km running are younger (with a mean of 37,63 years). Moreover, 10-km running has also the youngest and the oldest runners of 2016 edition, but there is a gap between 30-year-old runners and 40-year-old ones. Globally, runners seem to be more attracted by the challenge of longer distances (semi-Marathon and marathon) and the performance follows. From Wikipdia, we see that a large number of top marathon runners are 35 years old approximately. This is late enough to ensure that trainings of previous years are correctly exploited and sufficient when doing such running.

Performance analysis

Performance by age

The charts below show time distribution according to the age of participants and type of runnings. For every race, at the age of 60 years, median time starts to increase more steeply with age (with an exception for the runners of 64 years who do the marathon and who do better than the most of 25-year-old guys).

"The reality is, your max heart rate declines by about a beat a year."

As we can notice, young participants of 10-km running tend to be faster than the other ones and the time increases monotonically. Also, performance time for runners of the semi-marathon remains constant until 60 years. Finally, regarding marathon, the best runners are aged of 30-40 years. This confirms the hypothesis that marathon runners become better later compared to participants of runnings with lower distance.
Some explanations about the distribution of the performance of marathon can be found on Runner's World.

Statistical analysis

An interesting fact is that distributions seem to be quite homogeneous globally, while we observe real changes at the extremes. One can then ask if we can assume that the different categories of runners are similar or not, statistically speaking. To verify this assumption, we decided to use ANOVA, which is quite popular and useful when comparing multiple populations. More specifically, we used one-way ANOVA as in our case, we only have one factor to study over the different distributions.

Results: For certain groups, significant differences are observed (i.e. we can't affirm that all the age categories are similar during the different runnings).

Detailed methodoly and results

ANOVA requires at least three conditions to be respected. Those conditions are the following:

  • independence of the observations
  • normality of the observations
  • homogeneity/homoscedasticity (same variance for data in groups)

Clearly, the first condition is satisfied as the different categories of runners don't have nothing in common (people are different). What about the others? As Minitab explains in its blog, when it comes to analyze runnings, we can observe some kind of specific pattern in the data. Thus, we decided to plot the distribution according to the age categories to obtain a visual and immediate intuition about the type of distributions and check if we could assume normal distribution or not.

If we pay attention to the graphes, we see that sometimes, and even if we group carefully the data according to the type of races (10 km/semi-marathon/marathon) to avoid edge effects, distributions are not normal at all or suffer of heteroscedasticity, as explained in the article from Minitab quoted before. However, we observe that for each category and race, we have significant number of runners. Thus, we can apply the Central Limit Theorem. Notice that ANOVA is, by default and thanks to CLT, robust against normality assumption (and thus violation of such property is quite well tolerated).

Here are the two hypothesis we had for our one-way ANOVA:

  • H0 (null hypothesis): samples of all groups are drawn from populations with the same mean values
  • H1 (alternative hypothesis): mean values are different

As we can see, p-value is extremely low (<< 5%). Thus, we can safely reject H0. Put in other words, it means that we can assume significant differences between the different age categories (statistical differences between mean values). Such conclusion tends to confirm our initial assumption about the distributions presented before. But if ANOVA permits us to conclude about heterogeneity of runners' categories, it does not provide precise results. For this reason, we decided to also use Tukey HSD as post-hoc analysis.

Performance by sex

Strangely the average speed isn't so different between different races of Lausanne Marathon, while we could have expect that the speed of 10-km running would be higher because the distance is smaller, for example.
Moreover, the average speed for marathon was greater than the one of marathon of Paris, where the average speed was 2,55 m/s (well-done Lausanne :)).
Finally, the speed gap between men and women tends to decrease as distance increases. One fact can be that women begin a running if they are sure to be able to do it (cf. remark in demographic analysis' part), and do it correctly for sure.

Statistical results: Using ANOVA, we observed that difference between female and male runners is statistically significant. Detailed results are presented in the dedicated Notebook.

Performance by BIB numbers

We can recognize three major patterns following the BIB number of participants. These patterns are directly linked with the running categories. Moreover, we see that there is a correlation between the BIB number - and so with the estimated time - and the effective time of runners. Indeed, the higher the BIB number is, the lower the performance time is, globally. Notice that BIB numbers are given by the organisators of Lausanne Marathon themselves, and as we can read on the official website, each participant is asked to indicate his "estimated time of running for the attribution of start blocks", in oder to categorize the runners accordingly.

Important: As reader can see, it seems that there are some 'outliers' in the present graph. Among other things, we observe that there are some runners that appear in the marathon's category, while their time is inferior to the world record (2h2min57s). In fact, these runners are not participants of the marathon. This means that for some of them, BIB number is not directly linked to the performance, as it can be expected in general!

Team analysis

Lausanne Marathon allows runners to be in team. In the following part, we studied the effect of being in team instead of doing a running alone. The ratio between runners in team and individual runners seems quite equivalent between the different races available in Lausanne Marathon. However, teams are more present in the 10-km running. In fact, such distance seems to be more appropriate for the teams, as it is short enough to be a good challenge between beginner friends.

Now, regarding performance. In every race, median speed of runners in team is higher than the one of individual runners. However, and as we can see on the graph above, best performances come from individual runners. For people with "common" profile, to be part of a team seems to be a motivational factor. Also, we see that excellent runners prefer to do the race alone.

Statistical results: Using ANOVA, we observed that difference between runners who are part of a team and individual participants is statistically significant. Detailed results are presented in the dedicated Notebook.

As we can notice, most of people in team have finished the race in pair/group (mean difference time is about 10 minutes). But the maximum difference between the best and the worst performance times in a team is equal to 2h40. Notice that it is very unlikely that any two runners would have the exact performance time (within 15 seconds) unless they ran together. Thus, we can identify visual - but very accurate - clusters showing runners in pairs/groups.

Based on the graph, there are distinct differences that can be highlighted:

  • 10-km running: most people who are part of a team ran in pair/group (326 runners compared to 60 runners who have finished alone); the race seems to be more like a friend challenge (as we can notice with the important size of the different teams)
  • Semi-marathon: almost half of people in team ran in pair/group (162 pairs runners compared to 158 individual runners)
  • Marathon: the challenge seems to justify that we have more individual runners (51 runners finished alone, 17 runners did the marathon in pairs/groups)

Another interesting fact is that for the 10-km running, we have studied 105 female participants and 281 male ones, and among the 60 runners who have finished alone, there were 22 women and 38 men; this ratio (#male / #female) decreases by 1 point compared to the first one (2,7 to 1,7). For semi-marathon, we have studied a sample of 101 women and 111 men and among the 106 people who have done the race alone, 51 were women and 55 were men (i.e. the ratios are quite equivalent). One supposition could be that men who participate to the 10-km running in team didn't do it for competition and had more "team spirit" that kept them in groups, unlike women. Note that such proposition is derived from data of Lausanne Marathon and to make such definitive conclusion, we would still have to make in-depth analysis!
