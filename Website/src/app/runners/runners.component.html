Data pre-processing and overview

During the study of the marathon of Lausanne, we found runners for whom performances were different over the years for a given event. We have decided so to go further in our study and to focus on such runners in order to try to better understand the factors which can have an impact - direct or not - on the runners and their performances.
For instance, humidity could be one factor. Indeed, Polar indicates on its website:

"Your body tries to cool itself down during exercise, typically by sweating. Humidity reduces the effectiveness of sweating, which means your body temperature goes up – together with your heart rate."

Here, the main problem is that we don't have access to a lot of these factors like hydration, training, nutrition, altitude, and so one. In a first phase, we thus tried to ignore all of these by assuming that their effects are negligible in our case.

About methodology and data used

The present study was focused on the visualization of some following factors that could explain the performance gaps we saw:

  • age;
  • experience;
  • training*;
  • weather.

*Note that as we don't have such additional information about runners, we took number of km crossed per year during competitions and number of events per year.

Note, As we display the information about all races of our dataset some runners can appear multiple time.

After the processing of data, we obtained 47 runners. The group of considered runners had a mean age of 52,67 years, which is very high (but we have to keep in mind that part of data is related to 1999's events). Moreover in our dataset the frequent runners (more than 175 events) are quite old (mean birthyear = 1947 ) which could explains the high mean age of our dataset. Notice that all these runners have finished more than 10 races, for a total of 3535 races. Some of them have even participated in more than 500 events the past 17 years (i.e. more than 2 events each month in average). Put in other words, the majority of these runners are not amateur. Now, knowing that the more races you run in a year, the shorter the training period and the opportunity to obtain good trainings are, we wanted to check, among other things, if there was a possible correlation between the performance of a runner and the number of events he makes each year.

In any case, it is important to keep in mind that as everyone is unique and reacts differently, it is important to conduct an in-depth analysis on a bigger dataset to make fiable conclusions. For this reason, the results obtained in this study must be considered carefully and reader must put things into perspective.

Performance analysis

This part relies on marathons of Lausanne as they were marathons for which we obtained the most data.

From an individual point of view, we see that, generally, the cumulative distance and the total number of events remain constant through the years. Among other things, the the two best results have been obtained by different runners (i.e. runners with huge difference of number of kilometers crossed per year). Thus, the previous assumption we made about general factors is wrong and so we needed to individualize the study as much as possible. Doing that, some parameters did not change anymore among the manipulated data and so it has been unnecessary to consider some factors anymore.

Individual study

Methodology

To individualize the study, we computed some 'coefficients' for each interesting feature. Note that, for a given runner and a specific race, a coefficient's value is defined by the difference between the value of the considered factor (temperature, Humidity, and so on) for the aforesaid race and the value associated with the race during which runner obtained his best performance (i.e. value of considered race - value of best race). The idea behind such manipulation is to try to isolate as much as possible remaining external factors. For instance, if the coefficient related to temperature is positive for a given race, it means that the temperature was higher for this specific race than the temperature of the race where the runner obtained his best performance.

After that, we split the set of runners in two groups: the first group contained runners who have done more than 175 races the last 18 years and the second group contained runners who have done less than 175 races during the same period. Roughly speaking, the first group was about runners who race a lot, as participating to 175 events between 1999-2016 correspond to 10 races per year, while the second one was about amateur runners. Notice that the value 175 is totally subjective.

When all the processing of data was done, we applied the Shapiro-Wilk test on the two groups to compare the distribution of each coefficient with a gaussian. One can remark that in a perfect world, if the coefficient has no impact on the performance, then the aforesaid coefficient should follow a normal law, which means that the studied parameter improves the performance as much as it degrades it. More information can be found in the Notebook corresponding to the present part of the study.

Performance's coefficient

Regarding performance, we computed three parameters:

  • age coefficient (difference between the age of a considered year and the one of the year where the runner has obtained his best time);
  • event coefficient (difference between the number of events of the considered year and the one of the year where the runner has obtained his best time);
  • performance coefficient (difference between the total of kilometers crossed during the considered year and the total of kilometers crossed during the year where the runner has obtained his best time).

Weather's coefficient

Even if the races are usually done during the same period of the year, the difference of temperature can differ of 10 degrees (and so on for other weather data). Thus, we also decided to obtain detailed data regarding weather for the following races: Lausanne Marathon, 20km de Lausanne, Morat-Fribourg, Course de l'Escalade, Sierre-Zinal, Zermatt Marathon. The collected data were based on average results of weather census done by Infoclimat from 6:00 AM to 20:00 PM, for the exact day of each race.

We then computed three parameters:

  • wind coefficient (difference between the wind speed (km/h) of a considered year and the one of the year where the runner has obtained his best time);
  • humidity coefficient (difference between the humidity's percentage of a considered year and the humidity's percentage of the year where the runner has obtained his best time);
  • temperature coefficient (difference between the temperature (°C) of a considered year and the temperature (°C) of the year where the runner has obtained his best time).

Conclusion

We found that there are no big differences between experienced runners and inexperienced runners. We also found that runners obtain their best performance when they were younger (we recall that the mean age of the group of runners is 52,47 years) and when the temperature is warmer. More strangely, runners obtain their best performance when they race less kilometers than during the other years. There are some possible explanations of the phenomenon.

  • One explication could be that runners race more when they are getting older. For pure curiosity, we applied a regression test and we got a R-squared value equals to 0,0066. Thus, we can safely conclude that there is no linear link between these two variables (but it does not mean that there is no link at all).
  • Another hypothesis is that a lot of runners have probably participated to a lot of other sports' events not considered in our study (like triathlon/cycling/walking events for example). Note that such hypothesis seems to be confirmed as the observation we made disappears when we consider the event's coefficient.
  • Finally, such observation could result from the effect of an over-training of runners.

One can also remark another important aspect, which is the positive and negative impacts of the wind's coefficient. This is because the force and the direction of the wind can affect the performance of the runners!
