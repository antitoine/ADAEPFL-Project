Statistical analysis

An interesting fact is that distributions seem to be quite homogeneous globally, while we observe real changes at the extremes. One can then ask if we can assume that the different categories of runners are similar or not, statistically speaking. To verify this assumption, we decide to use ANOVA, which is quite popular and useful when comparing multiple populations. More specifically, we will use one-way ANOVA as in our case, we only have one factor to study over the different distributions.

ANOVA requires at least three conditions to be respected. Those conditions are the following:

  • independence of the observations
  • normality of the observations
  • homogeneity/homoscedasticity (same variance for data in groups)

Clearly, the first condition is satisfied as the different categories of runners don't have nothing in common (people are different). What about the others? As Minitab explain in its blog, when it comes to analyze runnings, we can observe some kind of specific pattern in the data. Thus, we decide to plot the distribution according the age categories to obtain a visual, immediate, intuition about the type of distribution and check if we can assume normal distribution or not.

If we pay attention to the graphes, we see that sometimes, and even if we group carefully the data according to the types of races (10 km/semi-marathon/marathon) to avoid edge effects, distributions are not normal at all or suffer of heteroscedasticity, as explained in the article from Minitab quoted before. However, we observe that for each category and race, we have significant number of runners. Thus, we can apply the Central Limit Theorem. Notice that ANOVA is, by default and thanks to CLT, robust against normality assumption (and thus violation of such property is quite well tolerated).

Here are the two hypothesis we have for our one-way ANOVA:

  • H0 (null hypothesis): samples of all groups are drawn from populations with the same mean values
  • H1 (alternative hypothesis): mean values are different

As we can see, p-value is extremly low (<< 5%). Thus, we can safely reject H0. Put in other words, it means that we can assume significant differences between the different age categories (statistical differences between mean values). Such conclusion tends to confirm our initial assumption about the distributions presented before. But if ANOVA permitted us to conclude about heterogeneity of runners' categories, it does not provide precise results. For this reason, we decided to also use Tukey HSD as post-hoc analysis.

Performance by sex

Strangely the average speed isn't so different between different races of marathon Lausanne, We could expect the speed of 10km faster because the distance is smaller.
The average marathon speed was greater than the marathon of Paris where the average speed was 2,55 m/s (let's go Lausannes :) )
The speed gap between male and female tend to decrease as distance increase, one fact could be that women don't begin the distance if they not sure to perform on distance. (cf : demographic point)

Team analysis

The marathon lausanne race allow runners in team, In the following study we are going to study the effect of team compare to individual runners and differenciate team members. The team:individual runners seems quite equivalent between the different races offered by the event. The teams are more present in the 10 km. The distance seems more appropriate for the teams, it's short enough to be a good challenge between beginner friends.

Speed median by distance and type of runners
Distance Individuals runners Team runners
10 km 3.02 m/s 3.11 m/s
20 Km 3.07 m/s 3.17 m/s

In every races the speed median of team runners is greater than individual runners but from the graph above the best performances come from individual runners. For "average" people, to be part of a team seems to be a motivational factor, compared to excellent runners who prefer doing the race alone.
